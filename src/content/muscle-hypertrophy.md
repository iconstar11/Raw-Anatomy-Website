## Key Facts (include numbers where possible)

### The 3 Mechanisms of Hypertrophy (Schoenfeld, 2010)

- **Mechanical Tension** is the primary and best-supported driver of hypertrophy. It refers to the force exerted on muscle fibers during contraction against resistance. Progressive overload (increasing load and/or reps over time) is the main way to increase mechanical tension. Eccentric (lengthening) contractions under load produce especially high tension and are strongly associated with growth.
- **Metabolic Stress** refers to the accumulation of metabolic byproducts (lactate, inorganic phosphate, hydrogen ions) during anaerobic work — the "pump." It likely contributes to hypertrophy *indirectly*: metabolite buildup causes local fatigue of low-threshold motor units, forcing recruitment of high-threshold (fast-twitch) fibers that otherwise wouldn't be activated at lighter loads. This explains why high-rep, low-load training (e.g., Blood Flow Restriction training) can produce hypertrophy comparable to heavy training when sets are taken close to failure.
- **Muscle Damage** refers to microtrauma in muscle fibers, primarily from eccentric loading and novel stimuli. The inflammatory repair response *may* contribute to hypertrophy via a supercompensation effect. However, current evidence suggests muscle damage is more a *byproduct* of effective training than a required driver — you can have growth without damage and damage without growth.
- Mechanical tension is the dominant mechanism; metabolic stress and muscle damage are secondary and potentially dependent contributors.

### Types of Hypertrophy

- **Myofibrillar hypertrophy**: Increase in the size and number of myofibrils (actin and myosin contractile proteins). Results in strength AND size gains. Favored by heavy, low-rep training.
- **Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy**: Increase in the volume of sarcoplasmic fluid and stored glycogen within the muscle cell, without adding contractile proteins. Increases muscle size but not relative strength. Favored by high-rep, moderate-load training. (Note: the distinction remains partially theoretical and is difficult to isolate in practice.)

### Cellular-Level Events: The 24–48 Hour Repair Window

**Immediately during/after training (0–2 hours):**
- Mechanical load activates mechanosensors in the muscle cell membrane (integrins, titin).
- mTORC1 (mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1) is activated — this is the master regulator of muscle protein synthesis (MPS).
- mTORC1 phosphorylates downstream targets **S6K1** and **4E-BP1**, which initiate translation of mRNA into new muscle proteins.
- Notably, early mTORC1 activation after exercise is **independent** of IGF-1/PI3K/Akt signaling — it responds directly to mechanical and nutritional signals.

**Phase 1 — Inflammatory/Immune Response (0–24 hours):**
- Damaged fibers release **cytokines** (inflammatory signaling molecules).
- **Neutrophils** arrive first (within hours) to clear cellular debris.
- Pro-inflammatory **macrophages (M1 phenotype)** accumulate to continue debris clearance and amplify the inflammatory signal.
- **Prostaglandins** (especially PGE2) are released; these moderate immune function and help activate satellite cells.
- This phase is responsible for DOMS (delayed onset muscle soreness), which peaks at ~24–72 hours post-exercise.

**Phase 2 — Satellite Cell Activation & Proliferation (~12–72 hours):**
- **Satellite cells** (muscle stem cells located between the basement membrane and sarcolemma) exit quiescence and re-enter the cell cycle.
- Activated by: mechanical signals, **IGF-1**, **HGF (hepatocyte growth factor)**, and prostaglandins from the inflammatory response.
- Satellite cells proliferate (divide), then **differentiate** and fuse with damaged fibers — donating new **myonuclei** to the fiber.
- More myonuclei = more transcriptional capacity = more potential for protein synthesis and fiber growth.
- The satellite cell pool can increase as early as **4 days after a single bout of exercise** and remains elevated with continued training.
- Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) also support this process by secreting growth factors that stimulate satellite cell expansion.

**Phase 3 — Anti-inflammatory Resolution & Protein Synthesis (~24–72 hours):**
- Anti-inflammatory **macrophages (M2 phenotype)** replace M1 macrophages; they secrete growth factors (IGF-1, TGF-β) that promote repair and remodeling.
- **Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS)** peaks at approximately **24 hours** post-resistance exercise — more than **double the baseline rate**.
- MPS returns to near-baseline (~within 14% of control values) by **36 hours**, with the elevated window lasting roughly **24–48 hours**.
- The actin and myosin myofibrillar proteins (the contractile machinery) are the primary proteins synthesized during this window.
- Net muscle growth requires MPS > MPB (muscle protein breakdown). Adequate dietary protein tips the balance toward growth.

### Key Molecular Players

- **mTORC1**: Master anabolic kinase; integrates mechanical, nutritional (amino acid/leucine sensing via Ragulator-Rag complex), and hormonal signals to drive protein synthesis.
- **S6K1 & 4E-BP1**: mTORC1 substrates that activate ribosomal translation machinery.
- **IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1)**: Produced locally in muscle; activates PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway; also critical for satellite cell proliferation and differentiation.
- **Testosterone**: Upregulates androgen receptors in muscle; enhances mTOR signaling and satellite cell activity. This is why young males with higher testosterone have greater hypertrophic potential.
- **PGC-1α**: Activated more by endurance exercise; promotes mitochondrial biogenesis rather than myofibrillar growth.
- **AMPK**: Energy sensor activated when ATP is depleted; can *inhibit* mTORC1 (catabolic vs. anabolic tug-of-war).

### Nutrition Numbers

- Daily protein for muscle growth: **1.4–2.0 g/kg body weight/day** for active individuals; some evidence supports **>3.0 g/kg/day** in resistance-trained athletes for body composition benefits.
- Per-meal protein dose to maximize MPS: **20–40 g** of high-quality protein, or ~**0.25 g/kg body weight per meal**, distributed every **3–4 hours**.
- Pre-sleep casein: **30–40 g** increases overnight MPS without disrupting fat metabolism.
- Skeletal muscle proteins turn over at ~**1.2% per day** in healthy adults.
- The "anabolic window" post-workout is not just 30–60 minutes; research suggests it extends to **5–6 hours** surrounding training (less critical if a pre-workout meal was consumed).

### Repeated Bout Effect

- After repeated exposure to the same training stimulus, muscle damage decreases significantly due to neural, cellular, and connective tissue adaptations — this is why DOMS lessens over time even as muscle growth continues.
- This means decreasing soreness over weeks of training is *normal* and does not indicate reduced effectiveness.


## Common Misconceptions to Address

- **"Soreness = muscle growth"**: DOMS is not causative of hypertrophy — it is a byproduct of muscle damage and the inflammatory response. Hypertrophy can occur with zero soreness (e.g., trained athletes), and extreme soreness (rhabdomyolysis risk) can actually impair training frequency and therefore total growth stimulus. The real driver is mechanical tension, not how sore you feel.
- **"You need muscle damage to grow"**: Muscle damage is neither necessary nor sufficient for hypertrophy. Studies show equivalent hypertrophy between damaged and non-damaged training conditions when mechanical tension is controlled (Flann et al., 2011; Peake et al., 2017). Damage and repair share some pathways with hypertrophy but are distinct biological processes.
- **"The pump causes muscle growth"**: Metabolic stress and cell swelling ("the pump") do not directly induce hypertrophy. A 2025 review (Sciencedirect) explicitly states: "Claims that metabolic stress, cell swelling or 'the pump' meaningfully contribute to hypertrophy are not supported by scientific evidence." Metabolic stress may facilitate hypertrophy *indirectly* via motor unit recruitment but is not an independent growth trigger.
- **"Lactic acid causes DOMS"**: Lactate is cleared from muscles within ~1 hour post-exercise. It does not cause DOMS (which peaks 24–72 hours later). DOMS is caused by the secondary inflammatory response to muscle fiber damage, mediated by immune cells and cytokines.
- **"The anabolic window is only 30 minutes post-workout"**: The anabolic window extends to 5–6 hours surrounding training. Immediate post-workout protein is mainly critical only when training in a fasted state.
- **"More training always equals more muscle"**: The repair and synthesis window requires 24–48 hours. Training the same muscle before the MPS window closes can impair net protein accretion. Frequency matters, but so does allowing adequate recovery between sessions for the same muscle group.
- **"Genetics don't matter much"**: Genetic variation in immune response robustness, satellite cell density, androgen receptor density, and mTOR signaling sensitivity causes large individual differences in hypertrophic response — some individuals show no measurable hypertrophy from resistance training programs that produce significant gains in others.
