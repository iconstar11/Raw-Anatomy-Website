# Overview

This research-backed guide explores the biological reality of muscle growth. We separate the "bro-science" from the peer-reviewed evidence to understand what actually happens at the cellular level during the 24–48 hour repair window.

## The 3 Mechanisms of Hypertrophy

According to the foundational work by **Schoenfeld (2010)** [1], there are three primary drivers of muscle growth:

1.  **Mechanical Tension:** The primary driver. Refers to the force exerted on muscle fibers during contraction against resistance.
2.  **Metabolic Stress:** The accumulation of byproducts (lactate, etc.) during anaerobic work — commonly known as "the pump."
3.  **Muscle Damage:** Microtrauma in muscle fibers, primarily from eccentric loading.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Key Finding:** Mechanical tension is the dominant mechanism; metabolic stress and muscle damage are secondary and potentially dependent contributors.

## Cellular-Level Events: The Repair Window

### Immediately Post-Training (0–2 hours)
Mechanical load activates mechanosensors which trigger **mTORC1**, the master regulator of muscle protein synthesis (MPS). This process is largely independent of IGF-1 signaling in its early stages [9].

### Inflammatory Response (0–24 hours)
Neutrophils and M1 macrophages clear cellular debris. This phase is responsible for **DOMS** (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness) [7].

### Satellite Cell Activation (~12–72 hours)
Muscle stem cells (satellite cells) proliferate and fuse with damaged fibers, donating new myonuclei to increase transcriptional capacity [2, 8].

### Protein Synthesis Peak (~24–48 hours)
**Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS)** peaks at approximately 24 hours post-exercise — often more than double the baseline rate [5].

## Common Misconceptions

*   **"Soreness = Growth":** DOMS is a byproduct of inflammation, not a requirement for hypertrophy.
*   **"The Anabolic Window is 30 mins":** The window is actually 5–6 hours surrounding training, and up to 48 hours for elevated MPS.
*   **"The Pump Drives Growth":** Recent reviews suggest "the pump" does not meaningfully contribute to hypertrophy independently of mechanical tension [10].
